Something Deeply Hidden by Sean Carroll

2020-09-12

Sean Carroll attempts to reconcile quantum mechanics with Einstein’s theory of relativity, something that physics conveniently ignores. There is a plethora of similar attempts such as Geometric Unity presented by Eric Weinstein. Carroll’s book is very readable even for physics enthusiasts like myself who lack formal education in the field.

Background:

  • Quantum probability
  • Schrödinger equation
  • Superposition
  • Many-Worlds theory
  • Electromagnetic field
  • Planck constant
  • Uncertainty principle, Heisenberg’s uncertainty principle

Quantum notes:

  • Qubits: systems with two possible measurement outcomes. A classical “bit” has just two possible values, 0 and 1. The state of qubit is a superposition of two possibilities (e.g. spin up and down), each weighted by a complex number
  • Entanglement arises because there is just one wave function for the entire universe, not separate wave functions for each component of the it
  • Decoherence is a situation when macroscopic objects become entangled with the environment. Decoherence causes the wave function to split into multiple worlds
  • “It makes no sense to wonder which branch I will end up in. Both of them have every right to think of themselves as me.” In Many-Worlds, the life-span of a person should be thought of as a branching tree
  • How many splittings have occured? Assume 10^(88) particules in the universe, each splits the wave function in two a million times per second, and we have 10^(18) seconds since the Big Bang: 10^(88) * 10^(6) * 10^(18) = 10^(112) splittings and 2^(10^(112)) branches
  • Special relativity (1905): everyone measures light to travel at the same speed in vacuum. Since there is no absolute frame of motion, time and space are relative
  • Consequences of General relativity: expansion of the universe and black holes. Special relativity is a framework, general relativity is a specific theory
  • Hawking: black holes emit particles into space. Those particles carry energy away from black holes. Black holes have entropy: where does the information go when black holes form and then evaporate?
